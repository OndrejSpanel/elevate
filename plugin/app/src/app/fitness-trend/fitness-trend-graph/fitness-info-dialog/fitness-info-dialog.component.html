About Multi-Sports Fitness Trend

The fitness trend is "Multi-sports" oriented as long as you hold a heart rate monitor and/or cycling. power meter and/or swimming activities having distance & time. The trend is calculated from your various heart rate stress, and/or cycling power stress and/or swimming stress given in your activities.

Heart Rate Stress Score (all workout types)

A heart rate stress is represented by a value called TRaining IMPulse or TRIMP. Training Impulse concept has been developed by Dr. Eric W. Banister. Basically, it represents the amount of heart stress during an activity: the longer you go at full throttle during an activity, the more TRIMP of activity goes up! TRIMP has both a positive and a negative effect. The positive effect is called fitness, and the negative effect is called fatigue. Fitness and fatigue can combined to provide a value of form & Performance.

Power Stress Score (cycling only)

If you own a power meter and you activated the cycling power meter option in the feature, then the "Power Stress Score (PSS)" will be used instead of TRIMP on cycling activities only if available.
Note: PSS is equivalent to Training Stress Score® (TSS®) from TrainingPeaks™. Learn more from TrainingPeaks™

Swimming Stress Score

A "Swimming Stress Score" (Swim SS) can be computed for each of your swim activities if your "Swimming Functional Threshold Pace" (Swim FTP) has been entered inside athlete settings and you enabled swimming stress score option. No heart rate monitor is required. Even a swimming manual activity will be handled by the system. Learn more from TrainingPeaks™.
Note: Strava do not support HR data for swimming activities at the moment. In the case where you have HRM while swimming and strava finally support HR data then TRIMP value will be used instead of Swim SS

Reading Curves

The Fitness, Fatigue and Form graph and table is based off an impulse-response model first developed by Dr. Eric W. Banister in 1975. It was later redefined by Andrew R. Coggan. This model will show you:
  • Your Fitness based on your chronic load
  • Your Fatigue based on your acute load
  • Your Form or stress balance
At a glance
  • The Fitness curve is the long-term average daily training load. Default period is fixed to 42 days (or six weeks), so it will take ~6 weeks for your Fitness to be accurate. It's basically the foundations, the potential availability of the athlete... You can't get into big rides/runs or races without a minimum of fitness acquired. If that curve is too regular or too flat, it is not usually a sign of good training.
  • The Fatigue curve is the short-term average daily training load. Same way than Fitness but default period is fixed to 7 days (one week). Conceptually, fatigue is easy to understand: It's the tired feelings which limits your performance. This curve varies much faster than Fitness curve. On Fatigue curve, you can see the fatigue climbing sharply in response where you performed workouts with a high stress. But also go down quickly as you take few days off.
  • The Form curve is simply the difference between Fitness and Fatigue, it's the right balance of stress you should look at to avoid over training for example.
    Form = Fitness - Fatigue
    When:
    • +25 < Form : Transition zone. Athlete is on form. Case where athlete has an extended break. (e.g. illness, injury or end of the season).
    • +5 < Form < +25 : Freshness Zone. Athlete is on form. Ready for a race.
    • -10 < Form < +5 : Neutral Zone. Zone reached typically when athlete is in a rest or recovery week. After a race or hard training period.
    • -30 < Form < -10 : Optimal Training Zone.
    • Form < -30 : Over Load Zone. Athlete is on overload or over-training phase. They should take a rest!
    • Read more from joefrielsblog.com
    Be fit doesn't mean you are ready to race at your higher level. A negative Form would reveal that you are carrying fatigue and are not on form. By resting you can remove fatigue faster than you lose fitness and come into form for your race day (or punish friend day... ;))!
